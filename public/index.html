Regional voice
Santa Barbara County is really three communication cultures — South Coast understatement, Valley plainness, and North County warmth-and-access. Each has its own psychology, and it changes how you write to donors there. Set the region on Test Your Message to score copy through that local lens.
South County — Santa Barbara
Santa Barbara · Montecito · Goleta · the South Coast

Deliberately apart from LA and proud of it. Sees itself as the custodian of something rare and finite, so the mental model is stewardship, not growth. Highly educated, hype-allergic, and quick to read past superlatives.

What drives them
  • Place is identity. Living here is a chosen affiliation, and giving is gratitude for getting to be here.
  • Quality over scale. Excellence at human size; suspicious of institutions that feel like they’re becoming something else.
  • We take care of our own. Isolation and the 2018 Thomas Fire and debris flow made self-reliance real, and Cottage was the safety net.
How to write to them
Understated, warm, literate, unhurried — a well-written letter from a trusted neighbor, never gloss or urgency. Excellence framed as an obligation, because there’s no backup 20 minutes away.
Spine: “This hospital is part of what makes this place possible.”
The Valley — Santa Ynez
Solvang · Santa Ynez · Los Olivos · Ballard · Buellton · Los Alamos

The real thing behind the postcard: agricultural, working, rooted. Judged by whether things work, whether they last, and whether you show up in person. Skeptical of being managed “over the hill.”

What drives them
  • Self-sufficiency. 30+ minutes over the pass from tertiary care; the hospital is infrastructure, not an amenity. They need to know it will still be there, still theirs, and still good.
  • Continuity. Multi-generational ranching families, the Chumash, Danish Solvang heritage. “Since 1964” beats “state of the art.”
  • Neighborliness as duty. Barn-raising culture; neighbors taking care of neighbors, not visionary transformation.
How to write to them
Plainer than Santa Barbara. Direct, concrete, short sentences, no gloss, dry humor. Real local names matter, and the community fact-checks them. Let distance and time (the 154 at night, fog, fire season) speak plainly.
Spine: “Your hospital, in your valley.”
North County — Santa Maria & Lompoc
Working California, reaching toward SLO and Ventura/Oxnard

Ag, food processing, logistics, Vandenberg and the military. Value is jobs, access, and respect for regular people, with a real, earned wariness of South Coast institutions “descending to help.”

What drives them
  • Access over aspiration. Trust is earned by whether I can get an appointment, whether someone speaks Spanish, whether my family is treated well without insurance.
  • Family and faith. Majority-Latino and multigenerational; family is the decision unit, and Catholic and evangelical leaders are the most credible messengers.
  • Broad-based giving. Church collections, employee giving at big ag and aerospace employers, and quieter relationship-driven major gifts.
How to write to them
Direct, warm, family-framed, and bilingual — Spanish as origination, not translation. Feature workers, parents, grandparents, and kids. South Coast understatement reads as cold here.
Note: SLO leans literate and civic like Santa Barbara but friendlier; Ventura and Oxnard mirror North County, while Thousand Oaks and Camarillo read affluent-suburban.

About this tool

This tool role-plays six fictional Santa Barbara–area donor personas built from Cottage Health Foundation audience research and the Foundation’s Voice of Philanthropy work. When you paste copy, pull a web page, or upload a PDF or image, each persona reacts in character and gives it a fit score. Every reaction is filtered through the same source material: the Foundation’s brand-voice calibration (the workshop sliders), the Tier 1 and Tier 2 word banks, the words-and-phrases-to-avoid list, and the photography principles. That’s why it rewards on-voice language, flags off-voice language, and looks for warmth, hope, local connection, and donor empowerment — it’s measuring your draft against a defined, consistent voice rather than one person’s opinion.

It’s built for evaluation and curation, not creation — a fast gut-check to pressure-test and refine messaging before it goes out, not a replacement for writing or for real conversations with donors. The personas are AI role-play and their feedback is directional, so the tool isn’t perfect and will sometimes get things wrong. Treat its scores and suggestions as a thoughtful second opinion, then use your own judgment.

How it weighs things. Not every signal counts equally. The score and the order in which fixes are surfaced follow this priority, set with the Foundation — most important first:

  1. Critical Inspiration over obligation — hope and possibility, never guilt or pressure
  2. Critical Community focus, outside-in — inclusive “we / our / us,” benefit to the community, not the institution
  3. High Warmth & humanity
  4. High Donor empowerment
  5. High Local relevance (coast to valley)
  6. High Avoids do-not-use language
  7. High Relationship-building over the ask
  8. High On-brand voice overall
  9. Medium Tier 1 core vocabulary
  10. Medium Tier 2 word banks
  11. Medium Clarity & readability

Critical criteria move the score the most; High criteria meaningfully raise or lower it; Medium criteria nudge it. The heat map shows these as columns so you can see exactly where a piece is strong or weak.

What AI gets wrong, and what to watch for

This tool is useful, but it is still AI, and AI has a predictable set of failure modes. Read every result with these in mind.

  • It makes things up. Hallucinated statistics, invented outcomes, and fabricated patient stories arrive looking exactly as confident as real ones. Verify every fact, number, and quote. This tool can never be cited as a source of anything.
  • It writes with tells. AI prose leans on symmetry and triads, uniform paragraph lengths, and a giveaway lexicon: delve, robust, leverage, journey, transformative, landscape, tapestry, testament, underscore, foster, seamless, and “it’s not just X, it’s Y.” Em dashes are now a widely known tell too.
  • It hedges. AI balances and qualifies where a human would commit. Writing that takes a position reads as authored; writing that carefully sees both sides reads as generated.
  • It is frictionless. Real institutions have tension: the campaign that stalled, the physician who pushed back, the gap between the vision and the building you actually have. Copy with no friction reads flat, and therefore false.
  • It writes about people, not from inside them. It describes an audience from the outside instead of writing from within a donor’s hesitation or a skeptic’s doubt.
  • It drifts to category language. Trained on a mountain of generic nonprofit copy, it reaches for “state-of-the-art facilities serving vulnerable populations” instead of the one witnessed detail that could only come from being there.
  • It carries bias. Its training data flattens and stereotypes. Watch it especially closely when it renders working-class, Latino, rural, immigrant, or patient voices, and when it quietly assumes what a “donor” looks like.
  • It is directional, not authoritative. The personas are role-play. Their reactions are a thoughtful second opinion, never evidence, and never a substitute for talking with real donors.
Using AI ethically in fundraising

There is no single binding standard yet, but two frameworks function as the sector’s de facto rules, and this tool is built to sit inside them.

Fundraising.AI’s Framework for Responsible and Beneficial AI is the most fundraising-specific. It was developed with input from more than 90 nonprofit sector experts, is reviewed biannually, and rests on ten pillars: privacy and security; data ethics; inclusiveness and bias mitigation; accountability, transparency, and explainability; continuous learning; collaboration; legal compliance; social impact; sustainability; and mission alignment. Three of its operating norms are adopted here verbatim: AI supports but never replaces human work, all public-facing content is reviewed by a human, and AI is not used to make decisions about people.

AFP’s Code of Ethical Standards does not name AI, but its existing standards govern it. Standard 12 requires that development marketing and communications be accurate, which means you own every claim the AI drafts. A hallucinated statistic or an invented patient story in a case statement is an ethics violation, not a tool error. The donor privacy standards matter even more here: donor and prospect information is the confidential intellectual property of the organization and may not be shared with or transferred to other entities, which most counsel now interpret to include pasting donor data into consumer AI tools that train on their inputs.

In practice, for case statements, internal docs, and email:

  • Accuracy accountability. Every fact, outcome statistic, and quote gets human-verified. AI cannot be cited as a source.
  • Donor data hygiene. No PII, wealth-screening data, or gift history into non-enterprise AI tools. Anonymize personas before using them as prompts.
  • Patient stories. In healthcare, never feed identifiable patient details into AI. HIPAA exposure sits on top of the philanthropy ethics layer.
  • Authenticity of voice. AI cannot fabricate a first-person testimonial, a signed letter’s sentiment, or a “personal” note that was never personal. A CEO letter drafted with AI assistance and genuinely reviewed and owned by the CEO is fine. A handwritten-feel donor note generated at scale is not.
  • Disclosure. The sector has not settled this. The working consensus: internal docs and standard comms do not require disclosure, but anything presenting itself as a person’s own expression does. The cleanest position is to disclose an AI-assisted process in your methodology rather than document by document.
How this tool writes

Because of all of the above, every suggestion and rewrite this tool produces is held to a human-voice standard. It varies sentence length hard, trades category language for witnessed detail, takes a position instead of hedging, lets paragraphs breathe unevenly, writes from inside the donor rather than about them, names the friction before resolving it, and never touches the AI lexicon or an em dash. It will not invent a statistic, a quote, or a patient story. Where a real fact is needed, it says so and leaves you a placeholder to go verify. The structure and the iteration are the machine’s. The judgment, the interviews, and the specifics are yours.
